When improvising individually

  1. Based on the bass line
  2. Based on the melody
  3. Bring in ideas from home
  4. Find two notes that sound good
  5. Repeat phrases with variations
  6. Based on the chord progression
  7. Playing/referencing a different song

When improvising collaboratively

  1. Using call and response
  2. Imitating/responding to others
  3. Starting with an emotion or theme
  4. Sharing support vs. melody role
  5. Round robin-style grooves
  6. Coordinating an opening (e.g. long tones, rhythms, chords)

How might I write up the jam bible?

Obvious Answer

Each "page" describes some structure you can use to facilitate jamming and improvisation with ideas for how to augment and “conduct” the jam. Specifically, each should include:

  1. Title
  2. Subtitle/description
  3. Necessary materials, space
  4. Optimal group size
  5. Description of how to get started, how it works
  6. Description of the stages of development, levels, facilitation notes, etc.

Nonobvious Answer

The bible is a collection of brief interviews with musicians about their favorite way to get started improvising or jamming. Musicians have all different background, levels of experience, levels of details of answers. Tips and tricks. Recorded examples/demos interspersed.

What directions might I go in from the loops workshop?

Post-Session Notes

  1. Exquisite Corpse sound recordings is good for fun, low stakes, games, etc.
  2. More ways to access/affect pitch/melody, there wasn’t a way to think about that except adding and subtracting layers
  3. Make a sound effect that would be used in a certain context (e.g. someone walking down stairs in a horror movie)
  4. Soundscapes might be easier to make than “songs” because they don’t require so many elements, just establishing a thing.
  5. How can you smoothly transition between things (e.g. increasing/decreasing reverb, audio, etc.)
  6. Could you do something that allows: Track A + Track B = Track C, now loop Track C
  7. Talking about tempo/rhythms, time signatures, etc.
  8. Using loops to create found soundscapes
  9. Perhaps actually watching scenes from movies or observing other contexts and composing “soundscapes” or “mood music” for those contexts.
